Introduction and Goals

Introduction to project background and client

At the beginning of our project, our client was Catherine Holloway. Catherine is a senior lecturer in the Department of Computer Science based in the UCL Interaction Centre. Catherine’s work is focused on the design of assistive technologies; she is currently working on a project called Power-Up!, which looks to design new power sources and interfaces for assistive technologies.

Our project stemmed from Catherine’s idea to help people with disabilities; the initial concept was to develop an activity tracker relevant to a specific sport, i.e. wheelchair basketball, to help wheelchair-bound people track their statistics during a game and also monitor their overall health.

However, due to unforeseen circumstances, our project and client changed. From this point, our client was Dr, Dean Mohamedally, a Principal Teaching Fellow for Applied Software Engineering and Industry Projects. Instead of requiring a particular user interface application for specific sensors, our new client requested a set of tools that could be used for the further development of assistive technologies. In this way, our client is now expecting a collection of smaller projects to retrieve, visualise and store data.

Project Goals

The aim of our project is to develop a set of tools that can visualise data, retrieve data from an API, generate mock data linked to the cloud, and display data on a user interface (mobile application). The tools will demonstrate to future developers the potential to integrate health related information in an application with the intent to maintain, improve and rehabilitate people with disabilities.

Our project has focused these tools on wheelchair basketball players, as this is where our initial project began with our first client. However the concept can be applied to to other sports and activities.

Our mobile application allows users to view data with the hope of encouraging players to improve their performance upon reflection of their statistics. This will in turn boost the general quality of wheelchair basketball across the team, not only increasing their chances of success in matches, but also improving their overall wellbeing.

As our project has progressed, every part of research was valuable despite not necessarily being relevant to the final set of tools we have produced. The information we gathered from requirement gathering helped us produce our end product, and therefore is both useful for the development of our tools and the future technicians who may use our tools. For this reason, it is worth noting the progression from the research we undertook with our initial set of requirements.

    Gathering Requirements: Meeting with End User

    At this stage, we knew what software and hardware the project required but needed to gather more information to deduce the requirements for the end product. To do this, we arranged a meeting with Pete Donnelly, a wheelchair basketball player who is part of a team that trains in Brixton. We spoke with Pete and witnessed a training session to discover the following information.

    Information Gathered about End User

    None of the users of the application will be elite athletes, so the the data doesn't have to be hugely precise. The users will be more concerned about the general improvement of their skills and health.

    The current wheelchair basketball players are mostly aged between 15-20. From this we can assume they will all be computer literate and familiar with using a smartphone. Additionally, players will not have age-related illnesses such as joint problems so there is no risk in terms of over-exertion in this sense.

    At present, all the wheelchair basketball players are male. However, it is possible for future members to be female so the application should be gender neutral.

    Desired Features

    Below is the list of desired features by the end user:

  • Connect to a phone to get stats / player profiles
  • Have an easy to use interface
  • Have an option to pause data collection (for time-outs etc)
  • Collect the following data: distance travelled over four separate quarters, average speed, top speed
  • Provide heart rate data
  • Monitor passes and shots
  • Provide calories burnt

    • Gathering Requirements: Surveys

    Our next step towards finalizing our requirements was to gather information through surveys. The following questions were asked so we could find the most common answer amongst candidates and produce an application suitable for most wheelchair basketball players.

    Analysis of Survey

    Question Analysis
    What type of phone/platform do you use? Half of our candidates use Android phones and the other half use iPhones so we should make our application compatible on one and then the other.
    Would you be comfortable wearing a fitband during trainings and matches? All candidates said yes so this could be an option to attach the sensor.
    How old are you? Most candidates are between the age of 16 and 31, so we can assume they have the technological experience to use a basic application.
    How long have you played basketball? Most players have played wheelchair basketball for at least four years. From this we can assume they have the ability to train and play with above basic ability such as moving and passing, so our application can include features that require these skillsets.
    Do you track any aspects of your game at the moment? Most candidates said they don't currently monitor any aspect of their gameplay, so any tracking of any statistics could help themimprove, giving plenty of possibilities regarding features.
    Would you like to track elements of your game? Most candidates said don't currently track any elements but all said yes to wanting to do so, so we can assume the users will utilise the application, and we can justify the want for it.
    Are there any aspects of your life you track? 87.5% of the candidates said they don't track any aspect of their lifestyle at all so we could add general health statistics as a feature of our application, that aren't necessarily specific to wheelchair basketball. This will help improve the players' overall health as well as encouraging them to use the application.
    Do you consider yourself disabled? 62.5% of candidates said they consider themselves disabled. Frequently with disabled people, this could imply they have related illnesses that aren't necessarily visible. For this reason it is imperative that our statistics are accurate to prevent the risk of any danger (such as incorrect reading of heartrate).

    Personas

    We created a number of personas to represent the client base who would use our application. In this way we were able to see the key functionalities our app would require to suite a broad set of users.

    Lily is a 36 year old who picked up wheelchair basketball as a means to remain fit after a car accident that left her paralysed from the waist down. She used to count her steps and kilometres ran but now finds it difficult to monitor her activity, and is worried that being in a wheelchair will cause her to put on weight. After each game she looks at the distance she covered during that game and how many calories she burnt. She adds the number of calories to the calories she burns through her metabolic rate and makes sure her calorie intake for the day is the same as or slightly less than this. She calculates the distance she covers over each month and sees that this maintains constant. As a result Lily feels just as fit before and doesn’t put on weight.

    Mike is a 30 year old man who has recently decided to take up wheelchair basketball in order to improve his health and fitness. However, Mike has never tried wheelchair basketball before and also finds it difficult to motivate himself to improve. He can’t see any improvements between games, and has no way to measure how hard he is trying so finds it easy to be lazy during a game and let the rest of the team do the work. In order to motivate himself, Mike starts measuring the number of passes he does and shots he takes compared to other teammates using the app. He finds himself at the bottom of the leaderboard for the first two games and decides to try harder in the next game. The number of Mike’s passes and shots begins to increase from game to game, and Mike also feels his fitness improve as he doesn’t feel as out of breath as he used to.
