Gathering Requirements: Surveys

Our next step towards finalizing our requirements was to gather information through surveys. The following questions were asked so we could find the most common answer amongst candidates and produce an application suitable for most wheelchair basketball players.

Analysis of Survey

Question Analysis
What type of phone/platform do you use? Half of our candidates use Android phones and the other half use iPhones so we should make our application compatible on one and then the other.
Would you be comfortable wearing a fitband during trainings and matches? All candidates said yes so this could be an option to attach the sensor.
How old are you? Most candidates are between the age of 16 and 31, so we can assume they have the technological experience to use a basic application.
How long have you played basketball? Most players have played wheelchair basketball for at least four years. From this we can assume they have the ability to train and play with above basic ability such as moving and passing, so our application can include features that require these skillsets.
Do you track any aspects of your game at the moment? Most candidates said they don't currently monitor any aspect of their gameplay, so any tracking of any statistics could help themimprove, giving plenty of possibilities regarding features.
Would you like to track elements of your game? Most candidates said don't currently track any elements but all said yes to wanting to do so, so we can assume the users will utilise the application, and we can justify the want for it.
Are there any aspects of your life you track? 87.5% of the candidates said they don't track any aspect of their lifestyle at all so we could add general health statistics as a feature of our application, that aren't necessarily specific to wheelchair basketball. This will help improve the players' overall health as well as encouraging them to use the application.
Do you consider yourself disabled? 62.5% of candidates said they consider themselves disabled. Frequently with disabled people, this could imply they have related illnesses that aren't necessarily visible. For this reason it is imperative that our statistics are accurate to prevent the risk of any danger (such as incorrect reading of heartrate).
